Public Safety Starts with Public Trust

Second Look Alliance is a nonpartisan research initiative dedicated to understanding what Americans truly believe about justice, accountability, and the possibility of change.

We survey residents across the country — Democrats, Republicans, and independents alike — on some of the most consequential questions in criminal justice: Who deserves a second chance? When do circumstances matter? What does a fair sentence look like? And does our system live up to the promise of equal justice under the law?

We do not advocate for a predetermined outcome. We follow the data. And what the data consistently shows is that public opinion on criminal justice is more nuanced, more compassionate, and more cross-partisan than conventional wisdom suggests.

Learn About Our Research

What We Do

Second Look Alliance conducts rigorous, population-representative public opinion research on criminal justice — and makes those findings available to the organizations working to improve public safety.

Our research is built on a straightforward premise: policy that reflects what the public actually believes is more durable than policy that runs ahead of or behind it. That means listening carefully, measuring accurately, and reporting honestly — even when the findings are complicated.

Our Research Approach

We use a combination of online surveys and advanced statistical modeling — including Multilevel Regression and Poststratification (MrP) — to produce population-level estimates that hold up to scrutiny. Our methodology allows us to examine public opinion at the state level with a level of precision that traditional polling rarely achieves.

Every survey we field is designed to get past the surface. We avoid leading questions and social-desirability framing. We test multiple ways of asking about the same issue so we can understand not just what people support, but why — and how stable that support is under different conditions.

What We Measure

Second Chances

The possibility of redemption and the conditions under which the public supports sentence review and reentry.

Circumstances of Crime

Domestic violence, trafficking, and coercion — when the context of a crime should shape how it is judged.

Access to Justice

Socioeconomic disparity in legal representation and the public’s view of economic fairness in the courts.

Proportional Punishment

Judicial discretion, mandatory minimums, and whether sentences fit the crime and the individual.

Across each of these areas, we look beyond simple approval ratings to understand the depth, durability, and cross-partisan reach of public support — giving our partners the information they need to work effectively and sustainably.

Supporting Organizations Dedicated to Public Safety

Criminal justice reform and public safety are not competing goals. The data shows that the public agrees — and Second Look Alliance exists to help organizations make that case with evidence.

We partner with advocacy organizations, legal aid groups, reentry programs, and policy research institutions that share a commitment to a justice system that is fair, effective, and accountable. We do not do their advocacy work for them. We give them the tools to do it better.

What We Offer Partners

Partnering organizations gain access to state-level public opinion data that is typically unavailable at this level of precision outside of well-funded statewide campaigns. Our research tells partners not just what the public supports, but which populations hold those views, how firmly, and where persuadable audiences are likely to be found.

We also provide context. A single data point is rarely enough. Our analysis places findings within the broader landscape of opinion across regions, demographic groups, and political identities — helping partners understand the full terrain they are navigating.

Who We Work With

Our partner organizations work across the full spectrum of public safety and criminal justice: legal defense organizations ensuring the right to counsel, victim advocacy groups focused on justice for survivors, reentry programs supporting successful return to community, policy institutes examining sentencing and corrections reform, and community organizations working on the root causes of crime.

What unites them is a commitment to an evidence-based approach and a belief that public opinion, properly understood, is an asset rather than an obstacle.

We are nonpartisan and non-advocacy. Our research is available to any organization working in good faith toward a more just and effective public safety system, regardless of political affiliation or ideological starting point.

What the Data Shows

Our surveys consistently find that Americans hold more thoughtful and nuanced views on criminal justice than the political debate often suggests.

The findings below reflect aggregate patterns across our active research states. They do not represent any single survey and should be read as indicative rather than definitive — the full data, methodology, and state-by-state breakdowns are available to partner organizations and researchers upon request.

Second Chances and Redemption

One of the most consistent findings in our research is broad public support for the idea that people can change. Across all six of our active research states, majority support exists for policies that allow individuals who have demonstrated genuine change over time to be considered for sentence review or early release.

This support is notably cross-partisan. While the language used to describe second-chance policies matters — and different framings activate different audiences — the underlying belief that incarceration should serve a constructive purpose is widely shared even among respondents who otherwise express skepticism about criminal justice reform.

Key finding: Majorities across party lines agree that a person who committed a crime years ago should not automatically be defined by that act for the rest of their life — provided they have taken genuine steps toward change.

Support is highest for policies affecting older incarcerated individuals who pose limited risk to public safety. It is more conditional — though still present — for younger individuals and those convicted of more serious offenses.

Expungement and record-clearing for lower-level offenses also commands significant public support, particularly when the case is made in terms of economic reintegration and community stability rather than leniency.

Domestic Violence and the Circumstances of Crime

Our research on domestic violence and crime circumstances addresses a fundamental question of justice: when someone commits a crime as a direct result of being abused, trafficked, or coerced, should the legal system take those circumstances into account?

The answer from the public is clear and consistent. Across all of our research states, large majorities of respondents — including those who generally oppose criminal justice reform — agree that the circumstances surrounding a crime are relevant to how it should be judged.

Key finding: Support for considering domestic violence victimization, trafficking, and coercion as mitigating factors in sentencing is among the highest we measure — consistently above 60 percent even among the most skeptical audiences, and significantly higher among the general population.

This finding holds across demographic and political groups. Respondents who describe themselves as strong supporters of law enforcement and tough sentencing are nearly as likely as reform-leaning respondents to agree that a person who killed an abusive partner after years of documented violence should be treated differently than someone who committed premeditated homicide.

The framing matters. When DV-related policy questions are presented in terms of the court system failing victims — rather than in terms of leniency for offenders — support increases substantially. This suggests that victim-centered language is not just more accurate; it is more persuasive.

Socioeconomic Disparity and Access to Legal Representation

Americans express strong concern about economic inequality in the justice system — and that concern crosses political lines in ways that may surprise those who follow the public debate.

80%+
Agree that people with more money receive better legal outcomes — across party lines

Public defender funding is one of the most broadly supported policy positions in our research. Framed as a matter of constitutional rights — the right to competent legal representation — it attracts support from respondents across the political spectrum who might otherwise be skeptical of reform.

Key finding: Adequate funding for public defenders is among the highest-support policy positions we measure, with strong bipartisan backing. It is also one of the few policy areas where support holds up robustly even among respondents who oppose broader criminal justice reform.

Fines and fees reform also draws significant public support. Respondents across the political spectrum are receptive to the argument that financial penalties that fall hardest on those who cannot pay amount to a form of double punishment — serving a jail sentence and then being unable to escape debt and consequences for years afterward.

Together, these findings suggest that economic fairness is a powerful and underutilized frame in the criminal justice conversation — one that connects with audiences who may not respond to other reform arguments.

Proportional Punishment and Judicial Discretion

Criminal sentences should fit the crime. This is not a controversial idea — it is one of the foundational principles of American jurisprudence. What our research reveals is that the public increasingly believes the current system fails to live up to it.

Large majorities of respondents agree that sentencing in the United States is often disproportionate — that mandatory minimums and rigid sentencing structures can produce outcomes that do not reflect the individual circumstances of a case, and that judges should have more latitude to ensure punishments fit the facts.

Key finding: Majorities across the political spectrum support giving judges more discretion to tailor sentences to individual cases — a finding that holds even among respondents who favor strict sentencing in general.

Sentence review — the idea that sentences handed down years or decades ago should be re-examined in light of current standards or changed circumstances — draws substantial support, particularly when framed around proportionality and the efficient use of public resources.

Taken together, these findings point toward a public that is more open to nuanced, individualized approaches to sentencing than the political conversation often assumes — and that sees judicial discretion not as a loophole, but as a safeguard against injustice.
